## 1. Title

Sanofi Is Building Its Own AI Ecosystem to Give the French Pharma Giant an Edge

## 2. Source

* Author / Organization: John Kell / Fortune CIO Intelligence
* Link: Fortune
* Date: 2026-05-27

## 3. One-line Summary

Sanofi is rejecting vendor-led AI agents and building an internal AI ecosystem around its own data lake, aiming to control workflows, reduce SaaS dependency, and automate enterprise operations.

## 4. Key Points

* Sanofi avoided broad deployment of ChatGPT Enterprise and Microsoft Copilot because leadership saw limited value beyond data security.
* The company built an internal generative AI assistant, Concierge, launched in October 2024.
* Concierge is used by 60,000 employees, roughly 80% of Sanofi’s workforce.
* The system connects to internal policies, organization charts, Workday, ServiceNow, SAP-related workflows, and enterprise data.
* Sanofi is moving from chatbot use cases toward agentic AI for IT, procurement, and sales.
* The company wants AI workflows to run on its centralized data lake rather than through disconnected SaaS vendor agents.
* Snowflake and Elementum AI are key partners in Sanofi’s agentic AI orchestration strategy.
* Sanofi expects AI agents to resolve up to 80% of IT employee requests and save about €10 million annually.
* Procurement automation may generate tens of millions in bottom-line savings.
* The strategy challenges SaaS vendors’ per-user pricing and vendor-controlled AI agent models.

## 5. Deep Dive — Structured Understanding

### Problem

Enterprise AI adoption often starts with generic tools such as ChatGPT Enterprise, Copilot, or SaaS-native AI agents.

For Sanofi, this creates three problems:

* High recurring cost
* Limited differentiation
* Vendor-controlled workflow logic

Generic AI assistants can improve productivity, but they do not necessarily transform core enterprise workflows.

### Approach

Sanofi chose to build an internal AI ecosystem instead of relying mainly on external SaaS AI features.

Its approach has three layers:

1. Internal AI companion
   Concierge acts as the employee-facing interface.

2. Centralized enterprise data
   Internal policies, organization charts, HR systems, IT systems, procurement systems, and SAP-linked workflows feed into a common architecture.

3. Agentic workflow orchestration
   AI agents are intended to operate across IT, procurement, and sales using Sanofi-controlled data rather than vendor-controlled agent chains.

### Key Insight

The strategic asset is not the AI model itself.

The strategic asset is the enterprise data layer and the workflow graph around it.

Sanofi’s leadership sees AI value coming from:

* Internal context
* Process ownership
* Data control
* Workflow redesign
* Company-specific automation

This shifts AI strategy from “buy better software” to “build an AI operating layer over company data.”

### Result / Impact

Sanofi claims meaningful operational impact:

* 60,000 employees using Concierge
* Potential automation of 80% of IT support requests
* Estimated €10 million annual IT savings
* Expected tens of millions in procurement savings
* Sales representatives may save about one day per week through AI-assisted preparation

The larger impact is strategic: Sanofi wants enterprise AI to become company-specific rather than SaaS-standardized.

## 6. Why It Matters

This article signals a shift in enterprise AI strategy.

The first wave of adoption focused on generic productivity tools. The next wave may focus on internal AI platforms that sit on top of company-specific data.

This matters because it could weaken the traditional SaaS model.

If large enterprises can orchestrate AI workflows directly from their own data lakes, they may rely less on vendor-specific AI agents from Salesforce, ServiceNow, Workday, or SAP.

The competitive advantage moves from software access to data architecture and workflow ownership.

## 7. Critical Analysis

Sanofi’s strategy is strong but not risk-free.

First, building an internal AI ecosystem requires high organizational maturity. Few companies have Sanofi’s scale, budget, governance, and data infrastructure.

Second, the article emphasizes projected savings, but many figures are estimates. Actual automation rates may be lower once edge cases, compliance, user trust, and escalation paths are included.

Third, replacing vendor agents with internal orchestration does not eliminate vendor dependency. Sanofi still relies on Snowflake, Elementum AI, Anthropic, OpenAI, Formation Bio, SAP, Workday, and ServiceNow.

Fourth, sales use cases involving doctor interactions and patient-related history require strict compliance controls. The article does not deeply explain privacy, auditability, or regulatory safeguards.

Fifth, the anti-SaaS framing may be overstated. Most enterprises will likely use a hybrid model: vendor systems for records, internal AI layers for orchestration, and foundation models for reasoning.

## 8. Connections

### 1. Data Lakehouse and AI Workflow Orchestration

Sanofi’s approach connects to the broader rise of lakehouse-centered AI architectures.

Snowflake, Databricks, and similar platforms are becoming control planes for enterprise AI because they centralize governed data.

### 2. SaaS Unbundling by AI Agents

The article reflects a possible unbundling of SaaS interfaces.

If AI agents can operate across systems, users may interact less with individual SaaS UIs and more with a unified AI layer.

### 3. Internal Enterprise Chatbots

Concierge resembles internal enterprise assistants used by large firms to answer policy, HR, IT, and operational questions.

The difference is Sanofi’s ambition to move beyond Q&A into workflow execution.

### 4. Agentic AI in ITSM

Automating IT support requests connects directly to IT service management systems such as ServiceNow.

This is one of the most realistic early areas for enterprise agents because workflows are ticket-based, repetitive, and measurable.

### 5. Pharma AI Transformation

Sanofi’s AI partnerships with OpenAI, Anthropic, and Formation Bio show that pharma AI is expanding beyond drug discovery into enterprise operations, sales, procurement, and IT.
