# Reclaiming Our Digital Selves: HCCF’s Vision for a Human-Centered Top-Level Domain

## Source

- **Author / Organization:** Human-Centered Computing Foundation (HCCF)
- **Link:** https://hccf.onmy.cloud/
- **Date:** 2026-06-29

---

## One-line Summary

HCCF proposes a new `.self` top-level domain to promote self-hosting and digital sovereignty, but the community questions whether its identity, governance, and economic model are technically and operationally viable.

---

## Key Points

- HCCF aims to create a new ICANN-approved top-level domain named **`.self`**.
- The project promotes **self-hosting**, **digital sovereignty**, and **human-centered Internet infrastructure**.
- Core principles include **one person, one domain**, free personal domains, and prevention of domain squatting.
- HCCF is participating in ICANN's Applicant Support Program (ASP) to reduce TLD application costs.
- The organization envisions shared infrastructure such as email and services tailored for personal-scale hosting.
- Community discussion quickly focused on implementation rather than philosophy.
- Major concerns include human identity verification, anti-abuse mechanisms, long-term funding, and governance.
- Many developers questioned whether a new TLD is necessary instead of operating under an existing domain.
- Ironically, HCCF's proposal was published as a PDF, drawing criticism from web developers advocating HTML-first documentation.

---

# Deep Dive (Structured Understanding)

## Problem

Today's Internet increasingly centralizes users' data and online identity within large platforms and cloud providers.

This creates concerns around:

- Platform dependence
- Data ownership
- Digital sovereignty
- Vendor lock-in
- Difficulty of personal self-hosting

---

## Approach

HCCF proposes a dedicated top-level domain (`.self`) optimized for personal Internet presence.

The proposal includes:

- Free personal domains
- One domain per verified individual
- Anti-squatting policies
- Infrastructure designed around personal rather than commercial usage
- Community-governed ecosystem supporting self-hosted services

---

## Key Insight

The proposal is less about creating another domain suffix and more about treating Internet identity as public infrastructure rather than commercial property.

Instead of optimizing for businesses, HCCF wants DNS policy itself to encourage personal ownership.

---

## Result / Impact

Although technically immature, the proposal generated extensive discussion because it challenges assumptions about:

- DNS governance
- Digital identity
- Self-hosting adoption
- Internet centralization

Most discussion shifted from the vision itself to whether the operational challenges are realistically solvable.

---

# Why It Matters

The proposal reflects a broader movement toward:

- Personal data ownership
- Self-hosting ecosystems
- Decentralized Internet infrastructure
- Privacy-preserving identity systems

Regardless of whether `.self` succeeds, it highlights growing dissatisfaction with platform-centric Internet architecture and renewed interest in user-controlled infrastructure.

---

# Critical Analysis

### Identity Verification

The proposal depends heavily on "one person, one domain," yet offers no concrete mechanism resistant to fraud, privacy concerns, or large-scale abuse.

---

### Governance

Many desirable properties—ethical technology, anti-abuse policies, domain revocation—ultimately require centralized governance, creating tension with the project's decentralization philosophy.

---

### Economic Sustainability

Operating a TLD involves substantial ICANN compliance, registry operations, and long-term funding.

The proposed donation/sponsorship model remains largely conceptual.

---

### Technical Differentiation

Critics argue most proposed functionality could already be implemented using existing domains (e.g., DuckDNS or delegated subdomains), raising questions about whether a new TLD is technically necessary.

---

### Practical Security

A domain explicitly signaling self-hosting may become an attractive target for vulnerability scanning, phishing, or automated attacks.
