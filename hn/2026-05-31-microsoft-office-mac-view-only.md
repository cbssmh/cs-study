# 3. One-line Summary



Microsoft will force certain perpetual-license Office for Mac installations into a view-only state after a licensing certificate expires, reigniting concerns about software ownership and subscription-driven business models.



# 4. Key Points



* Microsoft plans to move affected Office 2019 and some Office 2021 Mac installations into reduced functionality mode on July 13, 2026.

* Users will be able to open and view files but lose editing and saving capabilities.

* The trigger is the expiration of a license-validation certificate used by Microsoft Office.

* Office 2019 for Mac cannot receive the required update needed to avoid the issue.

* Microsoft previously stated that Office 2019 apps would continue functioning after support ended; later revisions removed this language.

* Affected users were offered migration paths including Microsoft 365 subscriptions and newer Office licenses.

* Community reaction was overwhelmingly negative, with many users describing the move as a violation of the expectations attached to perpetual licenses.

* Discussions revived interest in alternatives such as LibreOffice, OnlyOffice, and Apple's productivity suite.

* The incident highlights growing tensions between software ownership and recurring-revenue business models.



# 5. Deep Dive (Structured Understanding)



## Problem



Traditional software licensing assumes that a perpetual purchase grants indefinite access to core functionality. Microsoft's planned conversion of Office 2019 for Mac into a view-only product challenges that expectation.



The controversy is amplified because users purchased the product specifically to avoid subscription-based software.



## Approach



Microsoft relies on a certificate-based licensing validation system.



When the certificate expires:



* Updated products contain a renewed certificate and continue functioning.

* Older products that cannot receive the update enter reduced functionality mode.



Microsoft positions this as a technical licensing requirement rather than a deliberate product shutdown.



## Key Insight



The important issue is not the certificate itself.



The key issue is that a perpetual software product remains dependent on vendor-controlled infrastructure years after purchase.



This transforms ownership into a conditional relationship rather than a truly permanent one.



## Result / Impact



For users:



* Loss of editing capability.

* Forced migration decisions.

* Reduced trust in perpetual software licensing.



For Microsoft:



* Potential acceleration of subscription adoption.

* Reputational damage among power users and long-time customers.



For the industry:



* Renewed debate over digital ownership, right-to-use guarantees, and software preservation.



# 6. Why It Matters



* Demonstrates how software vendors can retain control over products sold as one-time purchases.

* Reinforces the broader industry transition from perpetual licensing to recurring subscriptions.

* Highlights growing consumer concerns around digital ownership and vendor lock-in.

* Serves as a case study for future regulation of software lifecycle management.



# 7. Critical Analysis



### Assumption: Certificate expiration makes the transition unavoidable



This is debatable.



Critics argue Microsoft could issue a final compatibility update or renew the mechanism rather than disabling functionality.



### Assumption: Users should simply upgrade



This overlooks the original value proposition of perpetual licensing, where future payments were explicitly unnecessary.



### Missing Context



* The actual number of affected users is unclear.

* Legal outcomes remain uncertain across different jurisdictions.

* Security and maintenance costs associated with indefinite support are rarely quantified.



### Potential Exaggeration



Some community commentary characterizes the event as "bricking" software.



Technically, the applications continue operating in a restricted state rather than becoming completely unusable.
